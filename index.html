Users weren't rejecting
the AI. They were abandoning it.

When an enterprise GenAI tool has an adoption problem, the instinct is to build more features. The data pointed elsewhere — to something smaller, and more fundamental: trust in the tool itself.

01
Signal Discovery

Three signals. One story.

Good VoC doesn't start with a survey. It starts with paying attention. The case for autosave was assembled from three independent sources — none of which were designed to surface this problem.

01 — 01 Qualitative

End-User Drop-in Sessions

Across multiple facilitated drop-in sessions, 2–3 users independently expressed frustration with losing work mid-session. These weren't formal feedback — they were off-hand remarks, the kind that get missed if you're not listening for them.

"I just close the tab at that point. I'd rather start again in Word."
01 — 02 Behavioural

SQL Telemetry — Abandoned Sessions

Session telemetry showed a pattern of abandoned deliverables at specific time thresholds — consistent with users losing unsaved work and disengaging rather than recovering. The timestamps corroborated what users were describing in the drop-ins.

Session drop-off clustering at 18–35 min — past the point of investment, before the point of completion.
01 — 03 Corroborating

Duplicate Deliverable Pattern

The same deliverable names, from the same users, appearing in close succession in the telemetry. Users weren't exploring — they were rebuilding. This was the clearest behavioural evidence of rework caused by data loss.

"It's happened twice now. I don't really trust it to hold my work."

Three independent signals — qualitative, behavioural, and corroborating — all pointing at the same failure mode. At this point, the question wasn't whether the problem existed. It was whether it was worth solving.

02
Commercial Frame

Making the cost of inaction visible.

The instinct in product conversations is to frame features in terms of what they add. This case was built in reverse — quantifying what inaction was costing, in terms the business already understood: billable time.

Using rate card data and telemetry, each abandoned session could be given a rough cost. Aggregate that across the user base and the frequency of abandonment, and the number becomes hard to ignore.

03
The Competing Priority Problem

The backlog wasn't empty.
It was pointed the wrong way.

The product team had a clear direction: build towards the AI-powered capabilities that were part of the broader platform promise. Autosave wasn't glamorous. It wasn't part of any demo. The challenge was reframing the conversation — not from features to features, but from adoption to value.

What the backlog prioritised

Higher-visibility AI features

The development focus was on capabilities that showcased the platform's AI potential — the kind of features that land well in steering committee updates.

AI-generated executive summaries
Natural language data querying
Automated anomaly / insight flagging
Generative proposal drafting
Embedded conversational assistant
vs
What the data was saying

Users weren't staying long enough to use any of it

Adoption was the primary commercial commitment to the client. Without users in the tool, the platform's AI capabilities were irrelevant. The most advanced feature in a tool no one trusts is worthless.

The argument wasn't that autosave was more important than AI. It was that autosave was a precondition for AI — you can't unlock value from a tool users won't stay in.

"You can't realise value from a feature users never reach. Adoption isn't a soft metric — it's the delivery vehicle for everything else we're building. We need to fix the floor before we can raise the ceiling."

04
The Recommendation

A hypothesis, not a feature request.

The recommendation to the product owner was framed as a testable proposition — not a gut feeling, but a structured argument built on triangulated evidence and a quantified business case.

The hypothesis
"If users trust the tool not to lose their work, they will stay in it longer — and adoption, our primary commercial metric, will follow."
The recommendation

Prioritise autosave above the next scheduled AI feature release. The cost of the fix is a one-time development investment. The cost of not fixing it is accumulating weekly, measurably, and directly undermining our adoption commitments to the client.

Evidence base: user drop-in observations, session abandonment telemetry, duplicate deliverable analysis, rate card commercial model.

05
Getting Buy-in

The path from evidence to decision.

The business case moved through three conversations — product owner, senior leadership, and the cross-discipline delivery team. Each required a different framing of the same core argument.

Stage 01 — Product Owner

Initial pushback on priority

The product owner's initial response was predictable: autosave wasn't in the roadmap, the sprint was already committed, and the AI feature pipeline had client visibility. The commercial model changed the conversation — not because the number was enormous, but because it was real. Quantified waste in a cost-conscious delivery context is harder to defer than a user complaint.

Friction: roadmap commitment, sprint velocity
Resolved via: commercial model + adoption risk framing
Stage 02 — Senior Leadership

Reframing for the room

Leadership weren't interested in a feature comparison. The message was stripped back: our AI-enabled delivery promise to the client is contingent on user adoption. Adoption is at risk. Here is the evidence. Here is the cost. Here is the fix. The autosave framing was replaced entirely — this was presented as an adoption intervention, not a technical patch.

Resolved via: adoption risk → client commitment narrative
Stage 03 — Cross-Discipline Delivery Team

Translating the case into requirements

With prioritisation secured, the role shifted to requirements translation — working across engineering, UX, and QA to ensure the implementation addressed the actual failure mode identified in the telemetry. The risk was building a technical autosave that didn't match the user's mental model of when and how saves occurred. Drop-in session insights were used directly to shape acceptance criteria.

Role: requirements translator, acceptance tester, user proxy
06
The Outcome

The hypothesis held.

Post-release telemetry and a follow-up drop-in session confirmed the expected pattern: session duration increased, abandonment rates fell, and the duplicate deliverable signature — the clearest signal of rework — dropped measurably.

The most significant feedback wasn't a metric. It was a behavioural shift.

07
Methodology Reflection

What this case was really about.

Autosave was never the point. The point was demonstrating that user insight, when properly triangulated and commercially translated, can move a product decision that pure intuition or stakeholder preference cannot.

The VoC methodology here wasn't a formal research programme — it was disciplined observation, systematic corroboration, and the willingness to build a quantified argument from signals others had dismissed as anecdotal.

In a context where AI adoption is both the promise and the pressure, the ability to identify and elevate the right problem — not the flashiest one — is where real value realisation work happens.
